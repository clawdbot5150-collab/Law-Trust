Types of Power of Attorney You Need to Know

Not all powers of attorney are the same. Here are the main types you might need:

  • Durable Financial Power of Attorney: Grants an agent authority over your finances — bank accounts, investments, real estate, taxes. The "durable" part means it remains valid if you become incapacitated. This is the most critical POA for estate planning.
  • Healthcare Power of Attorney (Healthcare Proxy): Names someone to make medical decisions if you can't. Different from a living will — this appoints a decision-maker rather than pre-specifying decisions.
  • Springing Power of Attorney: Only becomes effective when a specified condition is met (usually incapacity). Adds a layer of protection but can slow decision-making in emergencies.
  • General Power of Attorney: Broad authority over your affairs, but terminates if you become incapacitated. Used for temporary situations like travel or real estate transactions.
  • Limited/Special Power of Attorney: Specific authority for a defined transaction (e.g., selling a property while you're abroad).

For estate planning purposes, you almost always need a durable financial POA and a healthcare POA — one for finances, one for medical decisions.

What Powers Should Be Included in a Financial POA?

A well-drafted financial POA should specify exactly what powers your agent has. Look for services that allow you to customize these:

  • Banking: Access accounts, make deposits/withdrawals, manage safe deposit boxes
  • Real estate: Buy, sell, refinance, or manage real property
  • Investments: Manage stocks, bonds, retirement accounts
  • Tax filing: Prepare and file tax returns, handle IRS matters
  • Business operations: Run a business on your behalf
  • Gifts: Make gifts (important for Medicaid planning — must be explicitly granted)
  • Estate planning: Amend or create trusts, change beneficiary designations
  • Digital assets: Manage online accounts, cryptocurrency

Broad POAs are easier to use but carry more risk. Limited POAs provide less flexibility but more protection. Work with your chosen service to strike the right balance for your situation.

How to Execute a Power of Attorney Properly

Creating the document is only half the job. A POA that isn't properly executed is worthless. Here are the standard requirements:

  1. Principal must have mental capacity when signing — you must understand what you're signing
  2. Notarization: Required in most states. The principal signs before a notary public who verifies identity and witnesses the signature
  3. Witnesses: Many states require 1–2 witnesses who are not the agent or beneficiaries
  4. Recording: Not always required, but if the POA involves real estate, you may need to record it with the county recorder

Online services provide notarization guidance, and many offer online notarization through services like Notarize.com. Some states have specific statutory forms — using these "safe harbor" forms makes the POA more readily accepted by banks and institutions.

When Do You Actually Need a Power of Attorney?

The short answer: everyone over 18 should have a durable financial POA and healthcare POA. Here's why:

  • Incapacity from illness or injury: Even young, healthy people can become incapacitated suddenly. Without a POA, your spouse may not legally be able to access joint accounts, sell your house, or make medical decisions without a court order.
  • Aging parents: If your parents don't have POAs and develop dementia, you'll need a court-supervised guardianship or conservatorship — an expensive, time-consuming process that can cost $5,000–$20,000+.
  • Travel or deployment: A limited POA lets someone handle specific transactions while you're unavailable.
  • Real estate closings: If you can't attend a closing in person, a special POA authorizes someone to sign on your behalf.
  • Business continuity: A business owner POA ensures operations continue if you're incapacitated.

The cost to create a POA online ($9.95–$69) is trivial compared to the cost of not having one ($5,000–$50,000+ for court proceedings). This is one of the most cost-effective legal documents you'll ever create.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a durable power of attorney?
A durable power of attorney grants someone (your "agent") authority to manage your financial affairs. The "durable" designation means it remains valid even if you lose mental capacity — making it essential for incapacity planning. Without "durable," a POA automatically terminates if you become incapacitated, exactly when you need it most.
How much does an online power of attorney cost?
Standalone online POA documents cost $9.95–$69 per document, depending on the service. Most estate planning packages that include a POA range from $199–$499 total. Compare this to attorney fees of $200–$500 per POA document. Online services offer the same legal validity at 10–30% of the attorney cost for most situations.
Does a power of attorney need to be notarized?
In most states, yes — a durable financial POA must be notarized to be legally valid. Some states also require witnesses. Healthcare POA requirements vary more widely. Your online service will provide state-specific signing instructions. Online notarization is now available in most states through services that connect you with a commissioned notary via video call.
Can I revoke a power of attorney?
Yes. As long as you have mental capacity, you can revoke a POA at any time by creating a written revocation, delivering it to your agent, and notifying any institutions that have the POA on file. If you've used the POA for real estate, you may need to record the revocation with the county. Your original service should provide a revocation form.
What's the difference between a POA and a guardianship/conservatorship?
A POA is created voluntarily before incapacity. Guardianship/conservatorship is a court-ordered arrangement imposed after someone can no longer make decisions. The court process costs $5,000–$20,000+, takes months, and results in less control than a private POA. Creating a POA while you have capacity is far preferable to forcing your family through court proceedings later.
