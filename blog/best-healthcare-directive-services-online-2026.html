A healthcare directive is the document that speaks for you when you can't speak for yourself. It tells doctors and hospitals what medical treatments you want — or don't want — if you're incapacitated. Without one, your family may be forced into agonizing decisions without knowing your wishes, or medical providers may administer treatments you would have refused.

The good news: creating a healthcare directive online takes less than 20 minutes, typically costs under $50, and can save your family enormous emotional and financial strain. We've reviewed the top services to find the best healthcare directive services online in 2026.

Understanding Healthcare Directives: The Documents You Need

The term "healthcare directive" is often used loosely to cover several related documents:

  • Living Will: Written instructions specifying your medical treatment preferences — typically covering CPR, mechanical ventilation, artificial nutrition, and palliative care. Takes effect only when you can't communicate.
  • Healthcare Power of Attorney (HCPOA) / Healthcare Proxy: Names a specific person (your "healthcare agent") to make medical decisions when you can't. More flexible than a living will because your agent can respond to situations you didn't anticipate.
  • Advance Directive: Many states combine the living will and healthcare POA into a single document called an "advance directive." Some states have their own statutory forms.
  • POLST / MOLST: Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment — a medical order (not just a directive) for seriously ill patients. Created with your doctor, not an online service.
  • DNR Order: Do Not Resuscitate — a specific medical order signed by a physician. Not created through online services.

For most people, the key documents are a living will and a healthcare POA. Most online services provide both, often combined as an "advance directive."

What Should Be in Your Healthcare Directive?

A comprehensive healthcare directive should address these key medical scenarios:

End-of-Life Care Preferences

  • CPR (Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation): Do you want CPR attempted if your heart stops?
  • Mechanical ventilation: Do you want a breathing machine if you can't breathe on your own?
  • Artificial nutrition and hydration: Do you want feeding tubes or IV fluids if you can't eat/drink?
  • Dialysis: Do you want kidney dialysis if your kidneys fail?
  • Comfort care only: Instructions to focus on pain management rather than life-prolonging treatment

Healthcare Agent Powers

  • Authority to consent to or refuse medical treatment
  • Access to your medical records (HIPAA authorization)
  • Authority to hire/fire healthcare providers
  • Authority to make anatomical gifts (organ donation)
  • Authority to make disposition arrangements

Special Instructions

  • Religious or spiritual preferences that should guide treatment
  • Specific conditions under which you would want life support continued vs. withdrawn
  • Preferences about dying at home vs. in a medical facility
  • Mental health treatment preferences (psychiatric advance directive)

The more detail you include, the better. Vague directives leave more room for family disagreements and medical provider uncertainty.

How to Execute and Distribute Your Healthcare Directive

Creating the document is step one. Making sure it actually gets used when needed requires these additional steps:

  1. Execute properly: Sign before witnesses and/or notary as required by your state. Most states require 2 adult witnesses who are not your healthcare agent, family members, heirs, or healthcare providers.
  2. Give copies to your healthcare agent: They need to be able to present it quickly in an emergency.
  3. Give a copy to your doctor: Have it placed in your medical file with every provider you see.
  4. Keep a copy at home: In an accessible place where emergency responders could find it (not locked in a safe-deposit box).
  5. Register with your state: Many states have advance directive registries where you can file your directive. Healthcare providers can access it even if they can't reach you or your family.
  6. Carry a wallet card: A small card noting that you have an advance directive and where it can be accessed.
  7. Update your hospital records: When admitted to a hospital, present your directive for inclusion in your medical record.

The biggest failure mode with healthcare directives isn't creating a bad document — it's creating a good document that no one can find when it's needed.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a healthcare directive?
A healthcare directive (advance directive or living will) is a legal document specifying your medical treatment preferences if you become unable to communicate them. It typically covers resuscitation preferences, mechanical ventilation, artificial nutrition, and appointment of a healthcare decision-maker. It takes effect only if you lose the capacity to make decisions yourself.
What's the difference between a living will and a healthcare proxy?
A living will provides written instructions to medical providers — specific preferences about treatments. A healthcare proxy (healthcare POA) appoints a person to make decisions on your behalf. You need both: the living will gives guidance, while the healthcare proxy ensures someone with legal authority can advocate for you in unexpected situations. Many states combine them into one "advance directive" document.
Is an online healthcare directive legally valid?
Yes — online healthcare directives are legally valid when properly executed. Each state has specific signing requirements (usually 2 witnesses and sometimes a notary). Use a reputable service that provides state-specific forms, follow the signing instructions exactly, and distribute copies to your healthcare providers and agent. Validity depends on execution, not where the document was created.
How often should I update my healthcare directive?
Review your directive every 5–10 years, or sooner after: a serious illness or diagnosis, major surgery, a significant change in your health, a change in your healthcare proxy (death, divorce, estrangement), or a significant shift in your values or beliefs. Medical capabilities change too — treatments available today weren't possible 10 years ago. Regular review ensures your directive still reflects your wishes.
What happens if I don't have a healthcare directive?
Without a directive, doctors follow state law — typically defaulting to your spouse, then adult children, then parents. This can lead to family disagreements about your care, unwanted treatments, or decisions that don't reflect your values. Healthcare providers must err on the side of intervention without clear guidance. The resulting family conflict and emotional distress can be severe — preventable with a simple document that takes 20 minutes to create.
