What to Look for in an Online Will Maker

Not all will-making services are created equal. Here are the four things that actually matter:

1. State Compliance

Your will must meet your state's specific execution requirements — number of witnesses, notarization rules, and signature placement. Reputable services handle this automatically, but always verify your state is covered before purchasing.

2. What's Included

A complete estate plan isn't just a will. Look for services that include a healthcare directive (living will) and durable power of attorney. Trust & Will and LegalZoom both bundle these. Fabric's free tier only covers the will itself.

3. Update Policy

Your life changes. Your will should too. Check whether updates are free or cost extra. Trust & Will includes unlimited updates. LegalZoom charges per amendment. Nolo's software is one-time but requires buying the new version for major updates.

4. Attorney Access

If your estate is at all complex — blended family, business interests, assets over $500K — pay the extra for attorney access. LegalZoom and Rocket Lawyer both offer this. It's worth it for peace of mind.

When to Skip Online Services and Hire an Attorney

Online will makers work well for most people, but consider professional help if you have:

  • Assets exceeding $1 million (estate tax planning matters here)
  • A blended family or estranged relatives who might contest the will
  • Business ownership or partnership interests
  • Beneficiaries with special needs (wrong setup can disqualify them from government benefits)
  • Property in multiple states

Frequently Asked Questions

Are online wills legally valid in all US states?
Yes — online wills are legally valid in all 50 states when properly executed (correct witnesses, signatures, and notarization where required). Reputable services like Trust & Will and LegalZoom automatically apply your state's specific requirements.
How long does it take to make a will online?
Most people complete an online will in 15–45 minutes. Having your beneficiary information, asset list, and executor name ready beforehand speeds things up significantly.
What's the difference between a will and a living trust?
A will goes through probate (a public court process) and takes effect at death. A living trust is private, bypasses probate, and can manage assets if you become incapacitated. For estates over $150K, a trust often saves time and money for your heirs.
Can I update my will after creating it online?
Yes. You should update your will after major life events: marriage, divorce, the birth of a child, or significant changes to your assets. Check your service's update policy — Trust & Will offers unlimited free updates; LegalZoom charges per amendment.
What happens if I die without a will?
Your state's intestacy laws decide who gets your assets — which may not match your wishes. Courts also appoint a guardian for minor children. Creating even a basic will gives you control over these critical decisions.
