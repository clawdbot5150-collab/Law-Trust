How to Create a Will Online: Step-by-Step Guide (2026)

By Patricia Larson, J.D. • March 18, 2026 • 10 min read
Home / Blog / How to Create a Will Online

The quick answer: You can create a legally valid will online in about 20 minutes using services like Trust & Will. The process involves answering questions about your assets, beneficiaries, and wishes, then printing and signing the document according to your state's requirements. Online wills cost $50-$200 versus $500-$2,000+ for an attorney.

Creating a will doesn't have to be expensive or complicated. Modern online estate planning platforms make it accessible to everyone—but only if you do it correctly. This guide walks you through the entire process, from choosing a service to making your will legally binding.

Why Create a Will Online?

Online will creation has exploded in popularity because it offers:

  • Affordability – $50-$200 vs. $500-$2,000+ for an attorney
  • Convenience – Create a will from home in one sitting
  • Accessibility – No need to schedule appointments or take time off work
  • State-specific compliance – Reputable services ensure documents meet your state's laws
  • Easy updates – Many services allow unlimited revisions

According to a 2023 AARP survey, only 32% of Americans have a will—often citing cost and inconvenience as barriers. Online services remove those excuses.

⚠️ When to skip the online route: If you have a complex estate (business ownership, significant assets over $2M, blended family complications, special needs dependents, or tax planning concerns), consult an estate planning attorney instead.

Step 2: Gather Required Information

Before you start the online questionnaire, compile:

  • Personal information – Full legal name, address, date of birth, Social Security number (optional)
  • Executor choice – The person who will carry out your wishes (name, address, relationship)
  • Beneficiaries – Who inherits your assets (names, addresses, relationships)
  • Asset inventory – Real estate, bank accounts, investments, personal property (approximate values)
  • Guardian nomination – Who will raise your minor children (if applicable)
  • Specific bequests – Any particular items you want to leave to specific people (jewelry, heirlooms)
  • Alternate beneficiaries – Backup choices if primary beneficiaries predecease you
  • Witness information – Names of 2-3 people who can witness your signing (check your state's requirements)

You don't need exact dollar amounts for assets—general estimates are fine. The goal is to clarify your wishes, not provide a financial audit.

Step 3: Complete the Online Questionnaire

Most online services guide you through a series of questions organized by topic. Here's what to expect:

1 Personal & Family Information

Enter your name, address, marital status, children, and other dependents. Be accurate—errors here can cause problems during probate.

2 Executor Selection

Choose someone trustworthy, organized, and willing to serve. This person will handle your final affairs—paying debts, distributing assets, filing tax returns. Always name a backup executor in case your first choice can't serve.

Tip: Don't name co-executors unless they're extremely cooperative—it can cause delays and conflicts. Read more: How to Choose an Executor.

3 Beneficiary Designations

Specify who gets what. Options typically include:

  • Percentage distribution – "Split everything equally among my three children"
  • Specific bequests – "My vintage watch goes to my son Michael"
  • Residuary estate – "Everything else goes to my spouse"

Name alternate beneficiaries for every designation in case someone predeceases you.

4 Guardian Nomination (if you have minor children)

This is the most important decision for parents. Choose someone who:

  • Shares your values and parenting philosophy
  • Is financially stable enough to care for children
  • Is willing and able to take on the responsibility
  • Ideally lives nearby (to minimize disruption)

Critical: Discuss this with your chosen guardian before naming them. Surprising someone with this responsibility after you die is not ideal.

5 Special Instructions

Add any specific wishes—funeral preferences, organ donation, pet care, charitable donations, etc. Keep it clear and concise.

How Long Does It Take?

Most people complete the questionnaire in 15-30 minutes. If you have your information gathered beforehand, it's even faster.

Step 4: Review the Generated Will

Once you submit your answers, the platform generates a draft will. Read it carefully. Check for:

  • Spelling errors in names and addresses
  • Correct beneficiary designations
  • Proper legal language for your state
  • Inclusion of all your wishes
  • Executor and guardian nominations

Most services let you edit and regenerate the document multiple times before finalizing.

Step 5: Print and Sign Your Will Properly

This is the most critical step. A will that isn't properly executed is invalid—period. State laws vary, but most require:

General Signing Requirements

  1. You must be at least 18 years old (19 in some states)
  2. You must be of sound mind (mentally competent when signing)
  3. You must sign in the presence of witnesses (or acknowledge an earlier signature)
  4. Witnesses must sign in your presence and each other's presence

Witness Requirements by State

Most states require two witnesses. A few (Vermont, Louisiana) require three. Witnesses must:

  • Be at least 18 years old
  • Not be beneficiaries (or their spouses)—this is critical
  • Be mentally competent
  • Actually watch you sign the will

Some states also allow or require notarization to create a "self-proving" will, which speeds up probate by eliminating the need to track down witnesses later.

⚠️ Common mistake: Having a beneficiary witness your will can invalidate their inheritance in many states. Never use someone who stands to inherit as a witness.

Signing Ceremony Best Practices

  1. Print the will on regular paper (don't bind it—loose pages are standard)
  2. Gather your witnesses in one room
  3. Announce "This is my last will and testament" before signing
  4. Sign every page (or initial each page and sign the last page—check your state's rules)
  5. Have witnesses sign immediately after you, all in the same session
  6. If your state allows, have a notary present to notarize

Follow the state-specific instructions provided by your online service exactly. This is where people most often make mistakes.

Step 6: Store Your Will Securely

Once signed and witnessed, your will is legally valid. Now you need to store it properly:

Storage Options

  • Fireproof home safe – Accessible but protected
  • Bank safe deposit box – Very secure, but access may be restricted after your death (varies by state)
  • With your attorney – If you used one for review or other services
  • Digital storage – Some online services offer cloud storage (keep the signed original physical copy too)

Never store your will in a place where it might be destroyed or hidden by someone who benefits from your dying without one.

Tell Your Executor Where It Is

Your executor can't execute a will they can't find. Give them:

  • The location of the original will
  • Access instructions (safe combination, bank box key, etc.)
  • Copies of the will (though only the original is legally binding)

Consider giving copies (not originals) to your executor, attorney, or a trusted family member.

Step 7: Keep Your Will Updated

A will isn't a "set it and forget it" document. Review and update it after major life events:

  • Marriage or divorce – In many states, marriage revokes prior wills; divorce revokes provisions for ex-spouses
  • Birth or adoption of children
  • Death of a beneficiary or executor
  • Significant changes in assets (inheritance, home purchase, business sale)
  • Moving to a new state – Your old will may still be valid, but check state-specific rules
  • Changes in relationships – Estrangement from a named beneficiary, closer bond with someone new

Most online services let you update your will for free or a small fee. Learn more about updating your will.

How to Amend vs. Replace a Will

Small changes: Use a codicil (a legal amendment) for minor tweaks like changing an executor or adding a small bequest.

Major changes: Create a completely new will that explicitly revokes all prior wills. This is cleaner and less confusing.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Using Beneficiaries as Witnesses

In most states, if a beneficiary witnesses your will, they forfeit their inheritance. Use neutral third parties.

2. Not Signing in Front of Witnesses

You, the testator, must sign in the physical presence of witnesses (or acknowledge an earlier signature to them). Remote witnessing is allowed in some states post-COVID, but follow strict rules.

3. Forgetting to Update After Major Life Changes

A will that names your ex-spouse or excludes your new child creates enormous problems. Update promptly after life events.

4. Assuming Online Wills Work for Complex Estates

If you have business ownership, significant real estate holdings, or complex family dynamics, pay for an attorney. The upfront cost pales compared to the problems a DIY will can cause.

5. Not Coordinating with Beneficiary Designations

Assets with named beneficiaries (life insurance, retirement accounts, payable-on-death accounts) bypass your will entirely. Make sure those designations align with your overall estate plan.

6. Storing the Will Somewhere Inaccessible

If your executor can't access the will quickly, it causes delays and potential legal issues. Balance security with accessibility.

Are Online Wills Really Legal?

Yes—when executed properly. The American Bar Association recognizes that online wills are valid legal documents provided they meet state requirements. The key factors:

  • The document itself doesn't need attorney approval (though reputable services have lawyers review their templates)
  • Execution (signing and witnessing) must comply with state law
  • The testator must have capacity (be of sound mind)

Courts don't care whether you paid $50 or $5,000 for your will—they care whether it was properly executed.

Online Will vs. Attorney: Which Is Right for You?

Choose an online will if you have:

  • A straightforward estate (under $1-2 million)
  • Clear beneficiaries with no disputes expected
  • No business ownership or complex assets
  • A simple family structure (no blended families, special needs dependents, etc.)

Hire an attorney if you have:

  • Significant wealth (over $2 million)
  • Business ownership or partnership interests
  • Real estate in multiple states
  • A blended family or potential inheritance disputes
  • Special needs dependents requiring a special needs trust
  • Tax planning concerns (estate or gift taxes)
  • Desire for a living trust instead of just a will

You can also do a hybrid: create a will online, then pay an attorney $300-$500 to review it. This gives you affordability with professional oversight.

What to Include in Your Will

A complete will should contain:

  • Declaration – Statement that this is your will and revokes all prior wills
  • Executor appointment – Name and contact info, plus alternates
  • Guardian nomination – For minor children, if applicable
  • Beneficiary designations – Who gets what, with percentages or specific items
  • Residuary clause – What happens to everything not specifically mentioned
  • Alternate beneficiaries – Contingent heirs if primary beneficiaries predecease you
  • Signature and date – Yours and your witnesses'

Optional additions:

  • Funeral and burial wishes
  • Organ donation preferences
  • Pet care instructions and funding
  • Charitable donations
  • No-contest clause (discourages beneficiaries from challenging the will)

What NOT to Include in Your Will

Certain things don't belong in a will:

  • Assets with beneficiary designations – Life insurance, retirement accounts, POD accounts (these transfer outside the will)
  • Jointly owned property – Automatically passes to the surviving owner
  • Assets in a trust – Trusts have their own distribution rules
  • Illegal bequests – You can't leave assets contingent on illegal actions
  • Conditions that violate public policy – "You inherit only if you divorce your spouse" is unenforceable
  • Funeral instructions – While you can include them, wills are often read after funerals; better to leave instructions separately

Frequently Asked Questions

Are online wills legal and valid?
Yes, online wills are just as legally valid as those drafted by an attorney—provided they meet your state's requirements for execution. The key is proper signing and witnessing. Most reputable online services provide state-specific guidance to ensure compliance with local laws.
How much does it cost to create a will online?
Online will services typically cost $50-$200 for a basic will, or $100-$400 for a comprehensive estate planning package including healthcare directives and power of attorney. This is significantly cheaper than hiring an attorney ($500-$2,000+).
Do I need a lawyer to make a will?
No, you don't legally need a lawyer to create a valid will. Online services work well for straightforward estates. However, consult an attorney if you have a complex family situation, significant assets, business ownership, or tax planning concerns.
How do I make my online will legally binding?
To make your will legally binding: 1) You must be at least 18 years old and of sound mind, 2) Sign the will in the presence of witnesses (typically 2-3 depending on state), 3) Have witnesses sign in your presence and each other's presence, 4) Consider notarization if your state allows self-proving wills.
Can I update my will after I create it online?
Yes, most online services allow you to update your will as many times as needed. Major life events (marriage, divorce, birth of children, significant asset changes) should trigger a will review. Some services offer unlimited updates for a subscription fee; others charge per revision.
What happens if I don't sign my will correctly?
If your will isn't properly signed and witnessed according to state law, it may be deemed invalid. This means you'd die intestate (without a will), and state law would determine asset distribution instead of your wishes. Always follow your state's specific witnessing requirements exactly.
