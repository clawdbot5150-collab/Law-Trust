Two of the most respected names in legal self-help are LegalZoom and Nolo. LegalZoom is the online legal services giant that's processed millions of documents since 2001. Nolo is the company that literally invented legal self-help publishing back in 1971 — their WillMaker software has been a household name for decades.

In 2026, both offer online will creation, but with very different approaches, pricing models, and philosophies. Here's everything you need to know to make the right choice.

Quick Verdict

🏆 The Bottom Line

Choose Nolo WillMaker if you want the best long-term value (one-time payment, lifetime updates), prefer learning about estate planning as you create documents, or want downloadable software you own outright.

Choose LegalZoom if you want a comprehensive living trust package, attorney review options, or if you prefer a pure web-based experience without downloading software.

LegalZoom: The Market Leader

LegalZoom is the most recognized name in online legal services, period. Founded in 2001, they've processed over 4 million legal documents and have become the default choice for many Americans seeking legal help online. Their estate planning products are solid and well-tested.

LegalZoom Estate Planning Products

  • Last Will & Testament: $89 individual, includes digital storage and basic support
  • Living Trust: $279–$599 depending on complexity — one of the more comprehensive online options
  • Healthcare Directive: Available as standalone or bundled with will
  • Power of Attorney: Financial and healthcare POA available

LegalZoom's biggest advantage over Nolo is their living trust package. Their trust creation process is thorough, walks you through asset funding (the step most people miss), and includes a pour-over will automatically. If a living trust is your primary goal, LegalZoom is a stronger choice than standard Nolo WillMaker.

Nolo WillMaker: The Original Legal Self-Help

Nolo was founded in 1971 — thirty years before LegalZoom — by two legal aid lawyers who believed ordinary people deserved access to legal information. Their WillMaker software was first released in the 1980s and has been continuously updated ever since. That 50+ year track record in legal publishing carries real weight.

Nolo WillMaker Documents

  • Last Will & Testament: Comprehensive, state-specific, covers all standard scenarios
  • Financial Power of Attorney: Durable POA for financial matters
  • Healthcare Directive: Living will + healthcare POA combined
  • Healthcare Proxy: Designate someone to make medical decisions
  • Final Arrangements Document: Funeral and burial wishes (unique to Nolo)
  • Information for Caregivers and Survivors: Practical document organizing tool

Why the Lifetime Update Model Matters

Nolo WillMaker Plus is sold as a one-time purchase with lifetime updates. This means if your state updates its witnessing requirements next year (which happens), your software updates automatically at no cost. With LegalZoom, updating a previously created document may require paying again or subscribing.

For people who want to create their will, update it annually (which we recommend), and not pay subscription fees each time, Nolo's model is substantially more economical over a 5–10 year period.

The Educational Advantage

Nolo's background as a legal publisher shows in their documents. Every form includes plain-English explanations of what each provision means, why it matters, and what happens if you choose different options. If you want to understand your estate plan, not just create one, Nolo's experience as an educator is invaluable.

The Key Decision Points

Need a Living Trust? → LegalZoom

LegalZoom's living trust package is more comprehensive and better-guided than Nolo's trust products. If avoiding probate through a living trust is your primary goal, LegalZoom wins this category.

Want Long-Term Value? → Nolo

One purchase covers all your will updates indefinitely. If you're 35 and expect to update your will every few years for the next 30+ years, Nolo's one-time pricing model saves significant money.

Want Attorney Review? → LegalZoom

Nolo doesn't offer attorney review. LegalZoom's premium plans include attorney review options. If you want a human attorney to check your documents, LegalZoom is the clear choice of the two.

Complex Estate? → Neither

For complex estates — blended families, business ownership, real estate in multiple states, assets above $1 million — neither service fully replaces an estate planning attorney. See our guide on when a living trust is worth it for more context.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Nolo WillMaker as good as LegalZoom?
Nolo WillMaker produces equally valid legal documents and is backed by 50+ years of legal publishing expertise. For will and healthcare directive creation specifically, many users find Nolo's detailed explanations more educational than LegalZoom's interface. LegalZoom wins on living trusts and attorney access.
What is the price difference between LegalZoom and Nolo?
Nolo WillMaker Plus costs around $89.99 as a one-time download with lifetime updates included. LegalZoom charges $89–$99 for a basic will without updates. Over time, Nolo can be significantly cheaper since you pay once and get all updates for life.
Does Nolo WillMaker include a living trust?
Nolo offers a separate Living Trust product (Nolo's Online Living Trust or WillMaker & Trust software) that covers revocable living trusts. The standard WillMaker Plus focuses on wills, healthcare directives, and powers of attorney. LegalZoom's living trust product is available as a separate paid service.
Which is better for someone who wants to understand the process — Nolo or LegalZoom?
Nolo is better for people who want to understand estate planning as they go. Nolo was founded as a legal self-help publisher and their documents include detailed plain-English explanations for every legal concept. LegalZoom focuses more on efficient document creation.
Can I use Nolo WillMaker on any computer?
Nolo WillMaker is downloadable software for Windows and Mac. Nolo also offers an online version accessible via web browser. This differs from LegalZoom which is entirely web-based with no downloadable software component.
