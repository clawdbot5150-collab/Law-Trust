Joint Wills vs. Mirror Wills: What Couples Need to Know

Before choosing a service, understand this important distinction:

Mirror wills are two separate wills — one for each partner — that are identical in structure (you leave everything to your spouse, who leaves everything to you, with the same contingent beneficiaries). Each spouse can update their will independently without affecting the other's. Most estate planning attorneys and online services recommend this approach.

Joint wills are a single document covering both spouses. They're generally discouraged because once one spouse dies, the surviving spouse typically cannot change the will — creating serious problems if circumstances change. Avoid services that only offer joint wills.

All services on our list produce proper mirror wills (or "couples' plans" that are functionally mirror wills). None require a joint will format.

What Couples Should Include in Their Estate Plan

Essential Documents for Both Partners

  1. Last Will and Testament — Separate will for each partner designating the other as primary beneficiary
  2. Financial Power of Attorney — Each partner authorizes the other to handle finances if incapacitated
  3. Healthcare Directive — Each partner's medical treatment preferences
  4. Healthcare Proxy — Each partner names the other as healthcare decision-maker
  5. Guardian Designations (if you have minor children) — Critical and often the most important part

Optional but Recommended

  • Joint Revocable Living Trust — If you own real estate or have assets over $150,000, a living trust avoids probate for your heirs
  • Beneficiary Designation Review — Make sure 401(k)s, IRAs, and life insurance policies name the right beneficiaries
  • Letter of Instruction — Non-legal document with practical information for your executor

Special Scenarios for Couples

Blended Families

If either partner has children from a prior relationship, estate planning is significantly more complex. You need to balance providing for your current spouse with protecting the inheritance rights of children from prior relationships. Online services can handle basic scenarios, but complex blended family situations warrant an attorney review at minimum.

Unmarried Couples

Unmarried partners have zero automatic inheritance rights under most state laws. Without a will explicitly naming your partner as a beneficiary, your assets pass to blood relatives — not your partner. This makes estate planning more important for unmarried couples than married ones. Trust & Will and Rocket Lawyer both handle unmarried partner designations without issue.

Community Property States

If you live in Arizona, California, Idaho, Louisiana, Nevada, New Mexico, Texas, Washington, or Wisconsin — community property states — your assets are treated differently. Most online services handle community property automatically when you input your state, but it's worth understanding the basics. Frequently Asked Questions

Should couples get joint wills or separate wills?
Most estate planning experts recommend separate (mirror) wills rather than a single joint will. Mirror wills are identical in structure but separate documents for each partner, allowing either to be updated independently without requiring the other's consent. Joint wills create legal complications after the first spouse dies.
What is the best online will service for married couples?
Trust & Will's couples' package ($149) is our top pick — it creates complete mirror wills for both partners including healthcare directives and powers of attorney, at a price lower than creating two individual wills separately. LegalZoom and Rocket Lawyer are strong alternatives.
Do both partners need their own will?
Yes. Each partner should have their own individual will even if the content is identical (mirror wills). This ensures each person's estate plan is valid independently and can be updated without affecting the other's documents.
What should couples include in their estate plan?
Couples should have: two separate wills naming each other as primary beneficiary, two financial powers of attorney naming each other as agent, two healthcare directives, guardianship designations if they have minor children, and ideally a joint revocable living trust if they own real estate or significant assets.
Can unmarried couples use couples' estate planning packages?
Yes. Services like Trust & Will's couples' package are available to any two people in a committed relationship — married, domestic partners, or long-term partners. Unmarried couples often have even greater need for explicit estate planning documents since they lack automatic inheritance rights under most state intestacy laws.
